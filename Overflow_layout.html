Visible Overflow :

Default behavior: The content is not clipped, and it will display outside the container. Use cases: When you want the overflowing content to be fully visible. For simple layouts where clipping isn’t an issue. Considerations: Can lead to design inconsistencies if elements break out of their designated areas. Might cause unwanted overlaps in more complex layouts.





Hidden Overflow :

When you want to restrict content visibility strictly within the boundaries. Useful in fixed-size components like cards or modals. Considerations: It may hide essential content if not managed carefully. No scrollbars are provided, so users can’t access the clipped content.


Scroll Overflow:

What it does: Scrollbars are always provided (both horizontal and vertical) regardless of whether the content overflows. Use cases: When you need to ensure that users are aware that more content exists. In scenarios where constant scrolling options are preferred, even if not needed immediately. Considerations: Can lead to an unnecessary visual clutter if the content does not always overflow. May not provide the best user experience if scrollbars appear when not needed.


Auto Overflow :

Ideal for dynamic content sizes where overflow is uncertain. Balances between hidden (which clips the content) and scroll (which always shows scrollbars).
